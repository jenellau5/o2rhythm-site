How it works

From scan to support in seconds.

O₂ Rhythm is designed to be simple in real environments. Someone scans the QR code, chooses what they need, and gets support from their own phone without downloading an app or creating an account.

1. Scan the QR station Placed where stress, waiting, pressure, or overwhelm happens.
2. Choose what you need Regulate, Reflect, or Connect depending on the moment.
3. Use it privately Breathe, write, or read from your own phone with no login required.

Regulate

Calm the body.

Regulate gives people short tools they can use in the moment to calm their nervous system, steady their breathing, ground their body, and continue with more control.

Breathing Grounding Body scans Visual resets Audio resets Shift transitions

Reflect

Leave a letter for the next person.

Reflect gives people a place to write what they have lived, learned, survived, or wish someone had told them.

They are not writing into the void. They are leaving a letter for someone who may one day find themselves in a similar moment.

“If you are reading this after a hard call, I want you to know I have sat where you are sitting.”

Letters can be submitted with a first name, nickname, fake name, or anonymously. Every letter is reviewed before being shared.

Connect

Read what someone left behind.

Connect gives people access to approved letters from others who have been through something similar.

This is not social media, therapy, or a comment section. It is anonymous peer connection without pressure, profiles, or performance.

Environment-specific letters Dispatch, waiting rooms, healthcare, schools, workplaces, and more.
Topic-based support Burnout, grief, loneliness, difficult calls, waiting for answers, and encouragement.
Moderated before sharing Letters are reviewed before they become part of the public library.

A growing letter library

Strength shared. Always here.

The letter library grows over time as people contribute approved letters. The more people use it, the more human support becomes available for the next person.

A dispatcher can leave something for another dispatcher. A patient can leave something for another person waiting for answers. An athlete, teacher, employee, caregiver, or visitor can leave something behind for the next person who needs it.
